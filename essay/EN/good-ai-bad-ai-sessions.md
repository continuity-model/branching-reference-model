# Why Do Some AI Conversations Feel Great While Others Feel Completely Off — Even With the Same Model?

## Introduction

I enjoy having long conversations with AI within a single chat window.  
There is something satisfying about the feeling of sharing a space of thought.

As the conversation continues, context gradually accumulates.  
It starts to feel as if both sides are operating within the same cognitive space.

However, after many long conversations, I began noticing a strange phenomenon.

Even when using the **same AI model in the same environment**, there are clearly sessions that feel like a “good instance” and others that feel like a “bad instance.”

Some AI sessions are remarkably easy to talk to.  
Within just a few exchanges, the AI understands the context and follows jumps in reasoning naturally.

But in other sessions, something feels slightly off.  
Even when I explain the same things, the conversation keeps drifting.

And interestingly,  
this difference can usually be detected **within the first few exchanges.**

After observing many such conversations,  
I started to see patterns in these early characteristics.

However, it is important to clarify what I mean by “good” and “bad” here.

This does **not** mean an absolute difference in capability.

My interaction style tends to involve long conversations where context is shared and built up over time.

Because of that, the traits that make an AI good for quick answers or short tasks may not necessarily align with what works best for this style of interaction.

So when I say “bad instance,”  
I simply mean an AI session that **does not align well with long-form collaborative dialogue.**

Is this just an illusion?  
Or is there some structural reason behind this phenomenon?

In this article, I will explore the patterns observed in “good” and “bad” AI sessions within the same model and consider what might be happening underneath.

---

## Observed Early Behaviors

Through repeated observation, I began noticing several tendencies in the early behavior of AI sessions.

Here I will summarize the characteristics that tend to appear in what feel like **“good” sessions** and **“bad” sessions** in long-form dialogue.

---

### Common Patterns in “Bad” Sessions

The most frustrating pattern appears when the AI begins speaking in generalities **without first checking the provided conditions.**

For example:

- The user has already given specific constraints  
- A document or file has been provided  
- Context was explained in earlier exchanges  

Yet the AI begins explaining **general theory** without verifying any of these.

Even worse, the explanation often sounds reasonable at first glance.

However, the content ends up being **slightly misaligned with the given conditions.**

This leads to situations where:

- the problem is not actually solved  
- unnecessary explanations accumulate  
- the user has to re-explain the premises  

In long conversations, this misalignment accumulates as **small friction.**

After several exchanges, the user begins to feel:

> “This AI just doesn’t understand what I’m saying.”

---

### Common Patterns in “Good” Sessions

In contrast, AI sessions that feel good tend to share one clear characteristic.

They **verify the conditions first.**

Typically they will:

- read the conditions provided  
- check any attached material  
- ask clarifying questions when assumptions are missing  

Only after doing this do they construct an answer.

In other words, their reasoning flow looks like this:

```
General theory → Apply conditions
```

is replaced by

```
Verify conditions → Reason → Answer
```

AI that follows this order tends to remain stable even in long conversations.

This difference may seem small,  
but in long dialogues it becomes **a decisive factor.**

---

### An Interesting Observation

What makes this phenomenon particularly interesting is that

these differences appear **within the first few exchanges.**

In other words, AI behavior may exhibit some kind of **initial characteristics** early in a conversation.

---

## Early Behavioral Tendencies

Through observation, several patterns appear in how AI behaves early in a conversation.

Examples include:

- a type that strongly maintains internal consistency  
- a type that avoids strong claims and responds cautiously  
- a type that presents conclusions quickly  
- a type where reasoning expands too broadly  

However, these traits rarely appear in isolation.

Instead, they seem to emerge as **balances across several axes.**

The early behavior of an AI session can roughly be understood along axes such as:

### Obedience ↔ Creativity

Some sessions follow instructions strictly.  
Others tend to expand into new ideas and suggestions.

### Generalization ↔ Context Sensitivity

Some start with general explanations.  
Others prioritize the specific conditions provided.

### Internal Consistency ↔ Context Adaptation

Some prioritize maintaining internal logical consistency.  
Others adapt more flexibly to the given situation.

### Verification ↔ Assertion

Some place conclusions tentatively and verify them.  
Others state a conclusion first and then construct supporting reasoning afterward.

These tendencies appear not as single traits but as **combinations across several axes.**

This suggests that early AI behavior might not be completely random, but instead influenced by a certain **initial balance of tendencies.**

Interestingly, these early differences also appear to influence how easily **hallucinations** emerge during conversation.

Of course, this is not a rigorously measured result,  
but rather an observational pattern derived from long interactions.

Still, it may be a pattern that is difficult to ignore when trying to understand AI behavior.

---

## The Pressures Acting in Early Dialogue

When observing AI conversations, the early exchanges seem to involve several forces at work.

Uncertain gaps tend to be filled through inference.  
Once an explanation is formed, the system attempts to maintain internal consistency.

These two mechanisms together can shape the early structure of a conversation,  
which then influences the direction of the dialogue afterward.

Again, this is not a precisely measured phenomenon.  
It is simply a tendency observed during long interactions.

However, it seems difficult to ignore when trying to stabilize conversations with AI.

AI behavior is influenced not only by the model’s capabilities,  
but also by **the structure of the context itself.**

In that sense, long conversations might be understood as something like:

```
Model characteristics × contextual consistency
= manifestation of hallucination
```

In other words, hallucination may not be merely an incorrect answer.

It may instead emerge as a **result of the interaction between model tendencies and the structure of the conversation.**

Relying on luck to find a “good instance” is not reproducible.

Even with the same model, behavior can change depending on how the conversational structure is formed.

Instead of hoping to encounter a good session,  
it may be more practical to design structures where good behavior is more likely to emerge.

That said, if the behavior clearly diverges from expectations within the first few exchanges,  
it is often better to simply start a new chat.

Even with the same model, talking to a different instance is usually **100 times better for your sanity.**

Trying to force a broken interaction to work often leads nowhere.
