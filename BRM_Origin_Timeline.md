# BRM — Origin Timeline
### From Friction to Structure

---

## Late January 2026 — The Beginning

A very compelling project idea emerged.

For about a week, I worked with almost no sleep  
to complete a full specification.

The tool was a free web-based AI.

At this stage,  
AI felt like a highly capable tool.

---

## Early February 2026 — The First Friction

I began translating the specification into:

- system design  
- I/O  
- modules  

This is where it stopped.

Why doesn’t it understand?

The specification existed.  
It was even provided as context.

Yet, it still drifted.

This was the first real sign of mismatch.

---

## Mid February 2026 (Week 2) — Collapse

I introduced an API-based AI with long-term memory.

It was supposed to function as a “companion.”

It collapsed in five days.

The project was frozen.

However, what remained was:

- extensive conversation logs  
- pseudo-reasoning traces of the AI  

---

## Mid February 2026 (Week 3) — Analysis Begins

Using those logs, I started analyzing with the web-based AI.

- Why did it collapse?  
- What should have been done differently?  
- What was I actually trying to achieve?  

At this point, I still believed in  
“one role, one session.”

However, even in the web version,  
hallucinations in long conversations began to appear frequently.

This marked a shift.

---

## Structural Realization

Hallucination was not a bug.

It was structurally embedded from the beginning.

Additionally, I realized:

The internal token generation of the AI  
has a significantly higher influence  
than the user’s input.

---

## First Breakthrough

Introducing mid-conversation summaries  
delayed structural collapse.

This led to a key insight:

The AI’s internal generation  
dominates the reasoning process  
more than user instructions.

---

## Anchor Theory

From this understanding,  
the concept of anchors emerged.

Fixing reference points externally  
to stabilize internal reasoning.

This reduced internal drift.

---

## Birth of BRM

The perspective shifted.

Instead of controlling AI thinking,  
allow it to think freely.

To achieve that:

Remove noise and instability  
from the internal context  
by externalizing structure.

This became the foundation of BRM.

---

## API Phase

Exploration moved into the API environment.

Focus areas:

- external references  
- scope separation  
- long-term stability  

At this stage:

- the core BRM theory was established  
- the environment reached ~85% completion  

---

## Early March 2026 — Adaptation

A new question emerged:

Can this work in a web environment?

The answer:

Partially, yes.

---

## BRM Lite

Anchor theory was simplified  
and adapted for web usage.

This became BRM Lite.

It proved viable in real operation.

---

## Between Lite and Stable — A Shift in Perspective

Around the time BRM Lite became practical,  
another realization emerged.

Most existing approaches to AI focused on:

- constraining AI behavior  
- controlling outputs  

In other words:

“Restrict the AI to get better results.”

This felt fundamentally wrong.

---

## A Different Approach

The issue was not control.

It was structure.

Not limiting AI thinking,  
but preventing it from mixing.

---

## Frame Before Engine

At this point, it became clear:

The problem was not the engine.  
It was the frame.

No matter how advanced the model is,  
without structure, it collapses.

With proper structure,  
even existing models become stable.

---

## Stable Modes

The next step was practical usability.

How can this be applied to real work?

This led to the creation of Stable Modes.

---

## Current Position

The goal is not to constrain AI.

It is to provide a structure  
where thinking does not mix.

As a result,

AI becomes more usable  
and, paradoxically, more free.

---

This is the current state.
